Sugar in Potatoes

A report that looks at data taken from potatoes, and looks at their sugar content compared with the time they were stored for.

1 - Introduction

Potatoes are stored in bags after being harvested. This report will use data from 14 bags of potatoes intended for crisp manufacturing to analyse if the glucose content of potatoes can be predicted from the storage time. Glucose levels are measured in terms of mg per 100gms and the time of storage is measured in terms of weeks.

2 - Exploratory Analysis

2.1 - Summary Statistics

Figure 1: A scatterplot comparing the time the potatoes have been stored for against the glucose levels of the potatoes.

The glucose levels range from a minimum of 99 mg/100gms to a maximum of 245 mg/100gms, with a mean of 148.1 mg/100gms. The potatoes have been stored for 2-20 weeks with a mean storage time of 11 weeks.

2.2 - Initial Impressions

From the graph we can immediately see a quadratic shape in terms of the relationship between the two variables. Since there is a curve in the data, we can expect that the glucose content of a bag of potatoes can be predicted from knowing the storage time.

3 - Statistical Analysis

3.1 - Fitting the Linear Model

Figure 2: Table of summary statistics regarding the quadratic regression model.

Fitting the linear model in R we obtain Figure 2. It can be seen from this table that the equation of the quadratic line is: GLucose Content = 200.1693 − 19.4431 Weeks + 1.0304 Weeks2.

3.2 - Comments

Figure 3: The same scatterplot as Figure 1 with the regression line superimposed on the data points.

Looking at Figure 3, it appears as though the quadratic line fits the shape of the data fairly well. The R2 value is around 85%, echoing that the model is fairly representative of the relationship between the two variables. This shows that most of the variation in the glucose levels is taken into account.

3.3 - Predictions

Using the equation from section 3.1, we can try to predict the glucose levels of potatoes based on their storage time.

First let’s consider when the potatoes have been stored for 3 weeks. This would give a glucose content of 151.1136. Similarly, let’s consider a longer storage time of seventeen weeks. This gives a glucose content of 167.4222. Both of these results seem reasonable when thought about with respect to the existing data points.

4 - Checking Assumptions

4.1 - Standardised Residuals vs. Fitted Values

Figure 4: Scatterplot showing standardised residuals against fitted values of the regression model.

Figure 4 shows that the points are evenly scattered above and below the zero line. The points tend to be on the lower end of the fitted values. This suggests that we can assume the random errors have mean zero and that our regression model is appropriate.

In terms of vertical variation, there is much more variation in the values above the zero line when compared with those below. We therefore cannot assume the random errors have a constant variance.

The independence of the random errors can be assumed given the data was taken from different bags of potatoes.

4.2 - Normal Probability

Figure 5 shows that the points do not lie particularly close to the line of equality (y=x) and we therefore cannot assume that the random errors are normally distributed. This is echoed in Figure 6 showing the histogram of the residuals. It is clear from the graph that there is not a bell-shaped curve in the data, so normality cannot be assumed.

Figure 5: Normal probability (Q-Q) plot of the regression model.

Figure 6: Histogram of the standardised residuals obtained from the regression model.

Learnings From This Project

The main skill I gained from this report was using the different graphing functions on R, and how this allowed for different types of analysis.
